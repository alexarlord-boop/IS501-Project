\chapter{Phase 1 --- Methodology Evaluation}

\section{Overview}

Two dominant systems analysis methodologies are considered for FreshBakes
Bakery's web-based ordering platform: the \textbf{traditional Waterfall model}
and the \textbf{Agile (Scrum) framework}. Each carries distinct strengths
shaped by their core philosophy --- Waterfall enforces sequential discipline;
Agile embraces adaptive iteration.

\section{Structured Comparison}

\begin{longtable}{L{3cm} L{5.5cm} L{5.5cm}}
  \toprule
  \textbf{Criterion} & \textbf{Waterfall} & \textbf{Agile (Scrum)} \\
  \midrule
  \endhead
  \bottomrule
  \endfoot

  \textbf{Philosophy}
    & Linear, sequential phases; each phase completed before the next begins
    & Iterative sprints (2--4 weeks); continuous delivery and feedback \\[4pt]

  \textbf{Requirements}
    & Fully defined upfront; changes are costly and discouraged
    & Requirements evolve across sprints; change is welcomed \\[4pt]

  \textbf{Change Management}
    & Formal change-control process; high cost of late changes
    & Lightweight; changes incorporated in next sprint backlog \\[4pt]

  \textbf{Customer Involvement}
    & Heavy at start and end; minimal during development
    & Continuous --- Product Owner reviews every sprint demo \\[4pt]

  \textbf{Testing}
    & Deferred to a dedicated Testing phase near project end
    & Embedded in every sprint (Test-Driven Development encouraged) \\[4pt]

  \textbf{Documentation}
    & Comprehensive and mandatory at every phase gate
    & Lightweight --- just enough to support the sprint \\[4pt]

  \textbf{Team Structure}
    & Role-siloed (Analysts $\rightarrow$ Designers $\rightarrow$ Developers $\rightarrow$ Testers)
    & Cross-functional squads; shared ownership \\[4pt]

  \textbf{Risk Profile}
    & High --- failures discovered late; rework is expensive
    & Low --- risks surfaced and addressed sprint-by-sprint \\[4pt]

  \textbf{Delivery}
    & Single release at project end
    & Incremental working software after each sprint \\[4pt]

  \textbf{Predictability}
    & High timeline/cost predictability if scope is stable
    & Variable scope; predictable velocity per sprint \\[4pt]

  \textbf{Best Suited For}
    & Regulatory systems, embedded systems, fixed-scope projects
    & Customer-facing apps, evolving UX, start-ups, R\&D \\

\end{longtable}

\section{Specific Constraint Analysis}

\subsection{How Waterfall Handles ``Inventory Rules''}

FreshBakes' inventory logic is \textbf{rule-bound and deterministic}: a
confirmed Stripe payment triggers an immediate stock deduction; reorder alerts
fire at predefined thresholds; daily stock counts follow a fixed cycle. These
are stable, well-understood business rules that can be fully specified upfront
in a requirements document.

\textbf{Waterfall is appropriate here} because:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item The business logic does not evolve --- ``deduct 2 croissants per
        order'' is not subject to customer feedback.
  \item A complete data model (SKU, quantity, threshold) can be designed
        once and built without iteration.
  \item Rigorous documentation of inventory rules supports future audits
        and staff training.
  \item Any deviation during development is caught in a formal testing
        phase against pre-written acceptance criteria.
\end{itemize}

\textit{Weakness:} If a new inventory category (e.g., seasonal ingredient
tracking) is introduced post-design, Waterfall forces a costly change-request
cycle.

\subsection{How Agile Handles ``Custom Cake Design Iterations''}

Custom cake orders are the \textbf{antithesis of fixed requirements} --- a
customer may not know they want a fondant tier until they see a mockup; the
baker may suggest a flavour substitution mid-design; holiday themes change
weekly. Requirements here are inherently emergent.

\textbf{Agile (Scrum) is superior here} because:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item Sprint 1 can deliver a basic order form (flavour, size, message);
        customer feedback refines Sprint 2 into a visual design picker.
  \item The Product Owner (bakery owner) demos each sprint and redirects
        based on real customer reactions.
  \item A/B testing of UI layouts for the cake configurator is natural
        within a sprint.
  \item Features like 3D cake previews or decorator collaboration can be
        added to a future sprint backlog without disrupting current work.
\end{itemize}

\textit{Weakness:} Without discipline, scope creep risks delaying the core
ordering system. A well-maintained backlog and sprint velocity tracking
mitigate this.

\begin{lstlisting}[style=wireframe]

\section{Justified Recommendation: Hybrid Agile-First Approach}

\begin{verdictbox}
\textbf{Recommended Methodology:} Agile (Scrum) with Waterfall-style
governance for the Inventory \& Payment modules.
\end{verdictbox}

\medskip
\textbf{Rationale:} FreshBakes is a \textbf{small business undergoing digital
transformation} in a dynamic market (post-2025 tourism surge, evolving customer
expectations). The majority of the system benefits enormously from iterative
refinement with real user input. However, the \textbf{inventory and payment
subsystems} have fixed, auditable rules that justify upfront specification.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{L{4cm} L{3.5cm} X}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Module} & \textbf{Approach} & \textbf{Justification} \\
    \midrule
    Inventory management    & Waterfall (Sprint 0) & Fixed rules, auditable, no UX iteration needed \\
    Stripe payment          & Waterfall (Sprint 0) & Regulatory, deterministic, tested against spec \\
    Online ordering / UX    & Agile Scrum          & Customer-driven, evolving, needs real feedback \\
    Custom cake configurator& Agile Scrum          & Inherently iterative, high UX complexity \\
    Delivery route scheduling& Agile Scrum         & Optimisation logic improves with usage data \\
    Loyalty programme       & Agile Scrum          & Business rules will evolve with promotion cycles \\
    Admin dashboard         & Agile Scrum          & Baker/admin needs discovered through use \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabularx}
  \caption{Recommended methodology per system module}
\end{table}

This \textbf{hybrid model} is well-supported in the literature
(e.g., Boehm \& Turner, 2003) and is pragmatic for a small team operating
under budget and timeline constraints.

FreshBakes requires a standard web stack --- no exotic infrastructure. Cloud
providers (AWS, GCP, or DigitalOcean) offer Cypriot-region hosting with
\textasciitilde99.9\% SLA uptime, capable of handling 100+ daily orders

widely used in Cyprus e-commerce. A React/Next.js frontend + Node.js backend

interface can be delivered as a Progressive Web App (PWA) --- eliminating
app store overhead. No specialised hardware is needed; bakers and delivery
staff require only a smartphone or tablet.

\begin{verdictbox}
\textbf{Verdict:} Technically feasible with low complexity.

\section{Legal Feasibility}

Operating in Cyprus (EU member state), FreshBakes must comply with:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{GDPR (Regulation 2016/679):} Customer data must be collected
        with consent, stored securely, and deletable on request. The system
        must include a cookie consent banner and a privacy policy.
  \item \textbf{PCI DSS:} By delegating card handling entirely to Stripe
        (no card data stored server-side), FreshBakes remains out of PCI DSS
        scope --- a significant legal and operational simplification.
  \item \textbf{Cyprus Consumer Protection Law (L.81(I)/2013):} Order
        confirmation emails, return/refund policies, and transparent pricing
        are required for online sales.
  \item \textbf{eIDAS \& Electronic Signatures:} Order confirmations via
        email constitute valid electronic records under EU law.
  \item \textbf{Food Business Registration:} Already assumed compliant
        (existing bakery); the digital system introduces no new regulatory
        category.
\end{itemize}

\begin{verdictbox}
\textbf{Verdict:} Legally feasible with standard compliance measures.
\end{verdictbox}

\section{Why Agile is Superior for Delivery Route Optimisation}

Delivery route optimisation is one of the most compelling examples of why a
\textbf{pure Waterfall approach would fail} for this system.

\textbf{The problem with Waterfall here:} A Waterfall project would require
the team to fully specify the routing algorithm in Week 1 --- before a single
delivery has been made, before the team knows how many stops a typical day
has, and before knowing whether drivers navigate by address, landmark, or GPS.
Any upfront specification would be a guess, leading to a system built on
incorrect assumptions that is expensive to change.

\textbf{How Agile solves this iteratively:}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=wireframe]
Sprint 1  ->  Manual assignment by admin (address list only)
               | Real deliveries made. Insight: drivers prefer map view.

Sprint 2  ->  Google Maps link embedded per delivery
               | Insight: 3-5 deliveries per driver per day; clustering matters.

Sprint 3  ->  Route suggestions based on postal district clustering
               | Insight: drivers reorder stops themselves -- need drag-to-reorder.

Sprint 4  ->  Drag-and-drop route ordering + estimated time display
               | Validated by actual driver usage data.
\end{lstlisting}

Each sprint produces \textbf{working, usable software} validated by the
delivery staff who actually use it. The requirements for Sprint 4 could not
have been written in Sprint 0 --- they \textit{emerged} from real operational
experience.

This exemplifies Agile's core strength: \textbf{requirements are discovered
through use, not imagined in advance.} For a feature as operationally nuanced
as route optimisation, Agile is not just preferable --- it is the only
responsible approach.

\section{Design Effectiveness Assessment}

The system design addresses each of FreshBakes' original pain points directly:

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{L{3.5cm} L{5cm} L{3.5cm}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Pain Point} & \textbf{Design Solution} & \textbf{Requirement} \\
    \midrule
    Stockouts during peaks     & Real-time inventory deduction (SR-F01) + low-stock alerts (SR-F05) & UR-A02 \\
    Order mix-ups              & Unique order references (SR-F03) + digital baker queue (SR-F04)    & UR-B01, UR-B03 \\
    No loyalty tracking        & Loyalty Engine process (SR-F06, SR-F07)                           & UR-C07, UR-C08 \\
    Manual delivery scheduling & Delivery PWA with route optimisation (UR-D04)                     & UR-D01--D04 \\
    No online presence         & Full customer-facing web ordering system                           & UR-C01--C10 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabularx}
  \caption{Pain point to design solution traceability}
\end{table}

The \textbf{Agile-first hybrid methodology} proves its worth in Phase 4: the
delivery route optimisation could not have been fully specified upfront, yet
the ERD accommodates iterative enrichment of the \texttt{DELIVERY} entity's
\texttt{route\_notes} field without schema changes. The modular process design
(six discrete DFD processes) ensures each module can be tested independently
per sprint --- a direct architectural reflection of Agile's iterative delivery
principle.
